Introduction

As part of a class project, our group conducted an analysis on the performance of a brazed plate heat exchanger when used with heated and chilled water. In the image below, the heat exchanger is set to operate in a counter-flow configuration. We also took measurements regarding a parallel flow configuration.
  • For this setup, we used two submersible water pumps to pump water from resevoirs, through the heat exchanger, and into catch buckets.

Qualitative Notes

  • While running, the sides of the heat exchanger felt noticeably warm/cold to the touch, indicating that the heat exchanger was not truly adiabatic.
  • Additionally, counter-flow resulted in a greater temperature differential. Though it may be surprising that changing the directions of the flow would impact the efficiency of the heat exchanger so significantly, this makes sense, as in parallel flow, the two lines approach each other's temperature. However, in counter-flow, it is possible for each line to approach the original temperature the opposite reservoir.

Assumptions

  • I assumed that there was minimal change in velocity and elevation between the entrance and exit of the heat exchanger, resulting in minimal change in kinetic and gravitational potential energy
  • As noted above, there was more than one heat flow into the system. Heat likely flowed into the cold side of the heat exchanger, while simulanteously flowing out of the hot side. Unfortunately, my group did not take quantatative measurements of the temperature on each side of the heat exchanger, nor of the ambient air
    • Instead, in my diagrams and calculations, I generalized \(Q_{ambient}\) as a single term
    • I will explicitly calculate the value of \(Q_{ambient}\) to determine whether or not it is negligible
  • I modeled the liquid water as an incompressible substance, using \(c_p\) values from Table A-19 of "Fundamentals of Engineering Thermodynamics", interpolated to the average temperature
  • I modeled the system as steady state
  • I modeled the mass flow rate of both fluids as roughly equal, as they were being driven by identical pumps

\(Q_{ambient}\) Analysis

Parallel Flow
$$ \displaylines{ T_{c, avg}= 12.15 \; ^oC \qquad T_{h, avg}= 38.9 \; ^oC \\ c_{p, c} = 4.201 \frac{kJ}{kg*K} \qquad c_{p, h} = 4.179 \frac{kJ}{kg*K} \\ \text{Based on Figure 1,} \quad \Delta E = 0 = \dot{Q}_{ambient} - \dot{W} + \dot{m}_{c}(h_{c,in}+h_{c,out}) + \dot{m}_{h}(h_{h,in}+h_{h,out}) \\ W = 0, \, \dot{m}_c = \dot{m}_c \qquad \dot{Q}_{ambient} = \dot{m}((h_{c,out}+h_{c,in}) + (h_{h,out}+h_{h,in})) \\ \dot{Q}_{ambient} = \dot{m}(c_{p,c}(T_{c,out}+T_{c,in}) + c_{p,h}(T_{h,out}+T_{h,in})) \\ \frac{\dot{Q}_{ambient}}{\dot{m}} = 0.77 \frac{kJ}{kg*K} } $$
Counter Flow
$$ \displaylines{ T_{c, avg}= 14 \; ^oC \qquad T_{h, avg}= 23.3 \; ^oC \\ c_{p, c} = 4.159 \frac{kJ}{kg*K} \qquad c_{p, h} = 4.184 \frac{kJ}{kg*K} \\ \text{As above,} \quad \dot{Q}_{ambient} = \dot{m}(c_{p,c}(T_{c,out}+T_{c,in}) + c_{p,h}(T_{h,out}+T_{h,in})) \\ \frac{\dot{Q}_{ambient}}{\dot{m}} = -6.24 \frac{kJ}{kg*K} } $$
Interpretation
  • Compared to the heat exchange between the cold and hot water flows, \(Q_{ambient}\) is negligible. In future calculations, I will model the heat exchanger as roughly adiabatic

Performance Calculations

  • To account for the heat exchanger being not exactly adiabatic, I will calculate \(Q_{transfer}\) for both hot and cold and use the average when comparing parallel and counter flow performance
Parallel Flow
$$ \displaylines{ \text{Based on Figure 2,} \quad \Delta E = 0 = -\dot{Q}_{t, h} - \dot{W} + \dot{m}(h_{h,in}+h_{h,out}) \\ W = 0, \quad \dot{Q}_{t, h} = \dot{m}(h_{h,in}+h_{h,out}) \\ \dot{Q}_{t, h} = \dot{m}(c_{p,h})(T_{h,in}+T_{h,out}) \\ \frac{\dot{Q}_{t,h}}{\dot{m}} = 66.03 \frac{kJ}{kg*K} \\ \text{Similarly, through reversed,} \quad \dot{Q}_{t, c} = \dot{m}(c_{p,c})(T_{c,out}+T_{c,in}) \\ \frac{\dot{Q}_{t,c}}{\dot{m}} = 66.80 \frac{kJ}{kg*K} \\ \text{Finally,} \quad \frac{\dot{Q}_{t,parallel}}{\dot{m}} = 66.41 \frac{kJ}{kg*K} \\ } $$
Counter Flow
$$ \displaylines{ \text{As above, though based on Figure 3,} \\ \dot{Q}_{t, h} = \dot{m}(c_{p,h})(T_{h,in}+T_{h,out}) \\ \frac{\dot{Q}_{t,h}}{\dot{m}} = 87.86 \frac{kJ}{kg*K} \\ \text{Again,} \quad \dot{Q}_{t, c} = \dot{m}(c_{p,c})(T_{c,out}+T_{c,in}) \\ \frac{\dot{Q}_{t,c}}{\dot{m}} = 81.52 \frac{kJ}{kg*K} \\ \text{Finally,} \quad \frac{\dot{Q}_{t,counter}}{\dot{m}} = 84.69 \frac{kJ}{kg*K} \\ } $$

Interpretation

  • There was much more heat transfer in counter flow than in parallel flow
    • Quantitatively, there was 27.5% more heat transfer in counter flow than in parallel flow
    • This was despite the set-up being the same except for slight differences in the initial hot water temperature
